   claim, implementation-horizon, and external-grounding records.
8. Manifest/scaffold/proof/reader regeneration, full validation, local Quarto

## Candidate 1 — White-Box Evidence, Interpretability, and Activation Governance

**Proposed ID:** `white-box-evidence-interpretability-and-activation-governance`

**Placement:** Part IV, after `benchmark-ratchets-and-anti-goodhart-evidence`
and before capability thresholds.

**Exclusive job:** Govern when model-internal observations and interventions
may enter an evidence ledger or activation policy, with explicit
approximation, faithfulness, scope, side-effect, authority, and rollback
boundaries.

### Boundary

| Existing owner | Keeps | New chapter owns |
|---|---|---|
| Evidence States | Generic support states and transition discipline | White-box packet admissibility and inference ceilings |
| Adversarial Evaluation | Behavioral elicitation, sandbagging, monitor sabotage | Internal mechanism hypotheses and intervention validity |
| Replaceable Substrates | Adoption of architecture families | Interpretation/steering records across checkpoints and substrate changes |
| Security Kernel / Runtime Adapters | Permission to cause external effects | Permission to observe or alter activations and release a steered artifact |

### Core model

The chapter separates six states: feature discovery, human or automated label,
causal hypothesis, intervention result, activation policy, and deployed release.
A white-box record includes model/checkpoint identity, layer/site, input
population, extraction method, approximation error, feature stability,
pruning/label process, intervention, behavioral comparator, side effects,
uncertainty, authority, and maximum inference. Missing faithfulness or
out-of-distribution validity routes the record to residual or abstention.

### Failure families and strongest objection

- polysemantic, split, duplicated, dead, or unstable features;
- explanation laundering through readable graphs;
- reconstruction or replacement-model mismatch;
- prompt-local findings generalized to a model or deployment;
- intervention collateral effects and compensating pathways;
- evaluator/model/feature correlation; and
- interpretability access becoming a privileged attack surface.

The strongest objection is that present methods explain convenient proxies and
cannot carry governance weight. The chapter earns its place only if it encodes
that objection as admission tests and demonstrates at least one case where
white-box evidence adds calibrated predictive or diagnostic value beyond a
matched behavioral baseline.

### Evidence campaign

Use an open-weight model and preregister a small set of known or injected
mechanisms. Compare behavioral probes, probes/SAEs, and circuit/causal
interventions on held-out inputs. Measure mechanism localization, intervention
specificity, off-target behavior, stability across seeds/checkpoints, abstention
calibration, analyst time, and compute. Include scrambled labels, random
features, reconstruction-error stress, and adversarially selected prompts. A
negative claim cannot exceed N2 until the implementation and known-effect
positive controls pass; architecture-level claims require N4/N5 conditions.

## Candidate 2 — Governed World Models and Reality Grounding

**Proposed ID:** `governed-world-models-and-reality-grounding`

**Placement:** Part II, after `planning-as-a-control-layer` and before
`cognitive-compilation-and-semantic-ir`.

**Exclusive job:** Govern predictive state, imagined branches, model/world
disagreement, replanning, external correction, and action authority when an
agent plans through a learned or symbolic world model.

### Boundary

| Existing owner | Keeps | New chapter owns |
|---|---|---|
| Planning | Goals, DAGs, scheduling, replanning decisions | Predictive-state validity and imagined/observed discrepancy |
| Mathematical and Search Substrates | Candidate algorithms and search families | World-model lifecycle, error budgets, and grounding gates |
| Artifact Graphs | General provenance and replay | Observation/belief/prediction/branch/effect identities |
| Runtime Adapters | Effect authority | Whether modeled state is adequate to request that authority |

The chapter does **not** claim to solve philosophical symbol grounding and does
not absorb generic perception. It owns the operational bridge between admitted
observations, predictive state, imagined outcomes, and later observed effects.

### Core model

Every consequential rollout binds observation source and freshness, world-model
checkpoint, belief state, uncertainty, action-conditioned horizon, imagined
branch, planner/critic identity, prediction error, disagreement signal,
re-observation/replan trigger, action envelope, and residual. Observed effect is
never backfilled as though it were the prior prediction. Model error and policy
error remain separately identifiable.

### Failure families and strongest objection

- model exploitation and self-confirming actor/critic error;
- compounding rollout error and long-horizon overconfidence;
- rare-state erasure in compressed representations;
- causal confusion and off-policy failure;
- poisoned observations or feedback;
- branch leakage and simulated evidence laundering; and
- sim-to-real or model-to-deployment shift.

The strongest objection is that world models add a brittle hallucination layer
to a system that could plan directly. Admission therefore requires a matched
model-free or simpler predictive baseline and evidence that the model improves
the joint utility/grounding/risk/cost frontier in a favorable frozen regime.

### Evidence campaign

Choose a public environment with interventions, hidden state, rare hazards, and
distribution shift. Compare reactive, model-free, learned-world-model, and
oracle/symbolic upper-bound agents under matched interaction and compute. Freeze
prediction, calibration, intervention, rare-event, planner-exploitation,
recovery, and cost metrics. Run counterfactual and action-intervention tests,
world-model/critic disagreement, sensor corruption, horizon stress, and
out-of-distribution transfer. Independent environment truth must remain outside
the learned model/evaluator path.

### External source crosswalk

- `ext_world_models_2018` — compact predictive state and imagined training.
- `ext_dreamer_v3_2025` — modern broad world-model control comparator.
- `ext_v_jepa_2_2025` — representation prediction, MPC, and explicit planning
  limitations.
- MuZero remains a candidate comparator for planning-relevant rather than
  full-observation modeling; source-note it before chapter admission if used.

### Corben-source crosswalk

- `platonic_world_model` — world branches, grounding contracts, semantic
  transactions, disagreement, degradation, and rollback.
- `deterministic_capability_compilation` — dual-view world model,
  environmentalization, verification bandwidth, and effect-complete recovery.
- `qcsa_whitepaper` — particular/world-instance separation, grounding,
  open-world provisional objects, and typed uncertainty.
- `simulation_scaling` — simulation budgets and evidence boundaries.
- `cognitive_compilation` and `planforge` — handoff from predictive state to
  plans without transferring evidence or authority implicitly.

## Candidate 3 — Human Factors and Meaningful Control in Oversight

**Proposed ID:** `human-factors-and-meaningful-control-in-oversight`

**Placement:** Part I, after `human-intent-as-a-formal-input` and before
constitutional alignment.

**Exclusive job:** Determine whether assigned human oversight is actually
exercisable under the information, time, workload, competence, authority,
interface, incentives, and recovery conditions of the task.

### Boundary

| Existing owner | Keeps | New chapter owns |
|---|---|---|
| Human Intent | Formalizing what a principal requests | Human capacity to understand, contest, and control the execution path |
| Runtime Adapters | Approval and tool-permission enforcement | Quality and feasibility of the human role behind the approval |
| Scalable Oversight | Protocols for judging stronger systems | Human-factors validity, workload, reliance, and control degradation |
| Moral Uncertainty | Value conflict and contestability | Operational ergonomics of intervention and responsibility |

### Core model

An oversight-capacity record binds reviewer role and qualification, information
available, representation compatibility, decision time, concurrent load,
fatigue/alert state, authority and alternatives, conflict/incentive state,
confidence, intervention opportunity, outcome, appeal, and residual. The system
distinguishes nominal approval, informed review, effective intervention, and
meaningful control; none is inferred from a click.

### Failure families and strongest objection

- automation bias, complacency, misuse, disuse, and abuse;
- alarm fatigue, approval fatigue, and rubber-stamping;
- impossible monitoring duties and out-of-the-loop skill loss;
- responsibility without authority or ability;
- representation mismatch and explanation-induced overtrust;
- persuasion, urgency, and incentive manipulation; and
- reviewer surveillance or privacy harms created by measurement.

The strongest objection is that meaningful control is normative and cannot be
reduced to telemetry. The chapter must preserve that boundary while showing
that measurable necessary conditions can falsify ceremonial oversight and
improve concrete system design.

### Evidence campaign

Run an ethics-reviewed, minimal-risk study with representative consequential
review tasks, preregistered known defects, independent ground truth, and matched
interface conditions. Manipulate workload, time, explanation, automation
confidence, and intervention cost. Measure defect detection, calibrated
reliance, intervention quality, response time, workload, retention, and false
confidence. Include misleading explanations, high test-pass signals with hidden
defects, and forced abstention/fallback. Do not claim population or high-stakes
transfer without materially broader samples and independent replication.

## Candidate 4 — Governed Operations, Incident Command, and Graceful Degradation

**Proposed ID:** `governed-operations-incident-command-and-graceful-degradation`

**Placement:** Part IV, after `safety-cases-and-structured-assurance` and before
policy optimization.

**Exclusive job:** Own post-deployment observability, incident declaration and
command, containment, degraded modes, failover/manual fallback, recovery,
disclosure, learning, and decommissioning for AI systems as changing services.

### Boundary

| Existing owner | Keeps | New chapter owns |
|---|---|---|
| Readiness Gates | Admission, quarantine, and residuals before release | Continuity and control after admission |
| Safety Cases | Structured assurance claims and defeaters | Operational invalidation, incident action, and restored-state evidence |
| Artifact Graphs | General logs and replay | Incident timeline, command authority, containment, recovery, and disclosure |
| Capability Rollback | Replacement and state recovery semantics | Service-level degrade/failover/manual operation and incident lifecycle |

### Core model

An operational contract binds service/model/policy/dependency versions,
critical functions, impact tolerance, telemetry coverage, detector quality,
incident class/severity, declaration authority, commander and roles,
containment/degrade actions, rollback or compensation scope, human fallback,
communications/disclosure, evidence preservation, recovery objectives,
restoration tests, safety-case invalidation, lessons, and decommissioning.

### Failure families and strongest objection

- telemetry gaps and distributed-log fragmentation;
- alert storms, silent drift, and detector blind spots;
- authority ambiguity and competing incident commanders;
- rollback that misses optimizer/cache/descendant/external effects;
- failover to an equally compromised dependency;
- unsafe degraded modes or unusable manual fallback;
- premature recovery declarations and lost evidence; and
- incident learning that contaminates training or hides recurrence.

The strongest objection is that this is ordinary SRE material. The chapter is
warranted only because AI adds nondeterministic behavior, model/policy/data
identity, human-AI feedback loops, evaluator uncertainty, learning-state
mutation, and safety-case invalidation to the service-control problem. Generic
SRE practices remain baselines, not reinvented contributions.

### Evidence campaign

Build a bounded multi-process service with a real model or public local model,
external effect sandbox, distributed telemetry, explicit incident roles, and
stateful updates. Inject drift, corrupted inputs, model/version mismatch,
monitor failure, dependency failure, unsafe output, rollback failure, and human
capacity exhaustion. Compare stop-only, generic SRE, and governed-AI operations
under matched faults. Measure detection/containment/recovery time, safe useful
throughput, irreversible residuals, evidence completeness, operator load,
false alarms, governance cost, and recurrence. Recovery passes only after
independent outcome and state-integrity checks.
