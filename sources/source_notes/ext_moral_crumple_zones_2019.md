## Thesis

When agency is distributed and control is mediated through an automated
system, responsibility can be misattributed to a nearby human who had limited
effective control. Elish calls this pattern a moral crumple zone. The concept
supports a preliminary accountability-boundary question for human-AI
organizations; it is not an implemented organizational control or empirical
proof that a proposed allocation works.

## Mechanisms

- Analyze accidents involving complex automated socio-technical systems and
  the media coverage surrounding them.
- Locate responsibility across distributed agency and control mediated through
  time and space.
- Identify cases where the nearest human absorbs moral or legal responsibility
  while the larger technological system remains comparatively protected.
- Use the concept as a design and regulatory challenge, not as a tested control.

## Evidence

The reviewed abstract describes socio-technical case analysis and introduces a
conceptual framework. It does not report a controlled organizational study,
implemented delegation system, intervention effect, or local empirical result.
No accident reconstruction, legal determination, workforce study, or
accountability mechanism has been reproduced in this repository.

## Failure Modes

- Assigning responsibility to a person without matching authority, information,
  time, skill, or practical control.
- Treating a nominal human-in-the-loop as the cause or accountable owner of a
  system-level failure.
- Omitting designers, deployers, managers, vendors, and institutional incentives
  from the responsibility map.
- Turning a conceptual case analysis into a claim of legal liability or a
  validated governance solution.

## Claims To Add Or Update

- Require responsibility to be mapped against actual authority, information,
  timing, ability, and control rather than organizational proximity alone.
- Keep role assignment, delegation, intervention capacity, and post-incident
  accountability as distinct records in later chapter design.
- Do not infer manifest admission, legal compliance, control efficacy, or
  support movement from this abstract-only note.

## Open Questions

- Which organizational actors retain authority over objectives, deployment,
  monitoring, intervention, and recovery?
- What evidence shows that a named accountable person could actually prevent or
  change the outcome?
- How should affected workers and consumers contest a responsibility allocation?
