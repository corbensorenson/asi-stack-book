## Thesis

The paper describes a systemic risk pathway in which increasingly competitive
AI substitutes reduce the economic, cultural, and political dependence of
large-scale institutions on human labor, cognition, choice, and participation.
Human influence can then erode through ordinary local adoption pressure and
cross-domain feedback even without a sudden capability jump, coordinated
betrayal, or individually power-seeking system.

## Mechanisms

- Distinguish explicit influence, such as voting and consumer choice, from the
  implicit leverage humans receive because institutions still need human labor
  and cognition.
- Track relative and absolute disempowerment separately; aggregate prosperity
  can coexist with a falling human share of decision authority and resources.
- Model the economy, culture, and states as coupled systems. Economic power can
  shape culture and policy, while culture and policy alter adoption incentives
  and the distribution of economic power.
- Treat locally rational delegation, speed, scale, and competitive pressure as
  possible drivers even when no actor intends global disempowerment.
- Candidate indicators include AI shares of production and decisions,
  unsupervised AI spending, human control of major institutional decisions,
  the human/AI composition of influential culture, persuasive influence,
  AI roles in law, policy, and security, and cross-domain feedback loops.

## Failure Modes

- Slow substitution can remain below event-oriented warning thresholds while
  progressively removing human bargaining power and institutional dependence.
- Local adoption decisions can be individually rational yet compose into a
  system-wide loss of voice, contestability, and recovery options.
- Economic, cultural, and state feedback can amplify one another, so a narrow
  sectoral metric can miss the causal pathway that matters.
- Rising aggregate output can conceal relative human disempowerment when the
  distribution of resources and consequential decisions shifts toward AI-run
  institutions.
- Alignment to each deployer's stated intent can coexist with ecosystem-level
  outcomes that no affected human population selected or can reverse.
- Proposed indicators can become performative or Goodharted if treated as
  validated thresholds before causal and external-validity work exists.

## Evidence boundary

This is a conceptual and interdisciplinary risk argument, not a measured
trajectory or causal forecast. The candidate metrics are research proposals,
not validated leading indicators; the proposed intervention families are not
demonstrated mitigations. The paper itself emphasizes the absence of a concrete
established solution and argues that aligning individual systems to their
designers' intentions is not sufficient for ecosystem-level alignment.

## Claims To Add Or Update

- Do not define loss of control only as a sudden agentic takeover; monitor
  declining human option value, effective voice, resource command, and ability
  to contest or reverse institutional decisions over time.
- Require system-specific and interaction-effect indicators, prospective
  thresholds, human-welfare/agency outcomes, and counterfactual baselines.
- Preserve augmentation, displacement, distribution, feedback, and irreversibility
  as separate variables rather than collapsing them into deployment volume.
- Do not infer inevitability, a date, a probability, or mitigation efficacy
  from this source.

## Open Questions

- Which measurements are leading indicators rather than lagging descriptions?
- What thresholds distinguish useful augmentation from loss of meaningful
  influence, and who has authority to set them?
- Which interventions remain effective under international and market
  competition, and which merely move influence between systems?
- How can a controlled study identify cross-system causality without treating a
  toy economy, simulated polity, or short persuasion task as a civilization-scale result?
