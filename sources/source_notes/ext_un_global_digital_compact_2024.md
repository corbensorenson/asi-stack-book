## Thesis

The Compact treats AI governance as an international coordination problem that
also includes inclusion, capacity, interoperability, scientific assessment,
human rights, data governance, and multi-stakeholder participation. It is a
useful comparator for the institutional boundary the technical stack currently
lacks. An agreed compact does not demonstrate implementation or effectiveness.

## Mechanisms

- Intergovernmentally negotiated commitments with implementation milestones.
- An independent international scientific panel and a global policy dialogue.
- Coordination and compatibility of national and regional governance approaches.
- Capacity building, digital inclusion, international data governance, and
  participation by governments, technical communities, civil society, and firms.

## Evidence

The reviewed official pages establish the existence and stated scope of the
Compact and its follow-on institutions. They do not establish compliance,
enforcement, equitable representation, reduced race pressure, risk reduction,
or legitimacy in affected communities. Those are separate empirical questions.

## Failure Modes

- Treating international agreement as implemented control.
- Treating participation opportunities as representative legitimacy.
- Collapsing scientific assessment, political authority, standards, and
  enforcement into one institution.
- Inferring universal jurisdiction or legal obligations from a global framework.

## Claims To Add Or Update

- Model public authority, jurisdiction, participation, monitoring, enforcement,
  remedy, capacity, and coordination commitments as distinct records.
- Separate scientific consensus, political agreement, legal force, technical
  conformance, implementation, and observed public outcomes.
- Do not claim UN endorsement, compliance, legitimacy, safety, or support movement.

## Open Questions

- Which coordination commitments can be independently verified?
- How should conflicting jurisdictions and unequal state capacity be represented?
- What evidence can distinguish consultation from meaningful affected-party power?
