## Thesis

Decision authority changes incentives. Delegating to an AI or a human cannot be
decided from prediction accuracy alone because the allocation can change human
effort, learning, and the value of AI reliability.

## Mechanisms

- Model a principal allocating authority between a human and an AI.
- Trade off AI alignment/reliability against incentives for human information
  acquisition.
- Connect organizational design to how AI is trained and deployed.

## Evidence

The paper supplies a bounded economic model. It is not an empirical law about
organizations and does not settle accountability, legitimacy, competence, or
worker welfare.

## Failure Modes

- Automation bias and human deskilling.
- Nominal human authority without information or incentives to use it.
- Reliability investment optimized against the wrong organizational objective.
- Decision rights separated from accountability or remedy.

## Claims To Add Or Update

- Delegation records must include incentive and competence effects.
- Human-in-the-loop is not meaningful control by title alone.

## Open Questions

- Which organizational outcomes can be measured without reducing people to
  throughput?
- What triggers reassignment of authority when competence or incentives drift?
