## Thesis

A staggered deployment among customer-support agents provides bounded evidence
that AI assistance can alter productivity and distribute effects unevenly in a
real workplace. This supports measuring worker-level outcomes and heterogeneity
instead of inferring economic benefit from model benchmarks. One firm and task
family cannot establish general labor-market or ASI-transition effects.

## Mechanisms

- Staggered introduction of a conversational assistance tool.
- Worker-level operational outcomes over a large deployment population.
- Heterogeneity analysis across worker experience and performance.
- Examination of conversational practice, learning, and experience of work.

## Evidence

The paper reports a 5,172-agent setting and an average productivity increase in
that context, with substantial heterogeneity. The outcome is issues resolved per
hour within customer support, not employment, wages, ownership, bargaining
power, market concentration, welfare, or economy-wide productivity.

## Failure Modes

- Generalizing one workflow, firm, tool, and adoption period to the economy.
- Treating productivity as worker welfare, compensation, or durable skill.
- Hiding harmed or non-adopting groups inside an aggregate average.
- Confusing assistance effects with autonomous substitution or ASI effects.

## Claims To Add Or Update

- Separate task productivity, job composition, employment, wages, skill,
  worker experience, customer outcomes, ownership, and distribution.
- Require heterogeneous and delayed outcomes before transition claims.
- Do not claim economy-wide effects, worker benefit, or support movement.

## Open Questions

- Do productivity gains persist, diffuse, or change bargaining and compensation?
- Which workers gain skills and which become dependent or displaced?
- How do firm-level gains alter prices, entry, concentration, and public welfare?
