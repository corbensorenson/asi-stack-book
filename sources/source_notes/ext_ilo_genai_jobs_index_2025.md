## Thesis

Occupational exposure is heterogeneous across tasks, occupations, countries,
income groups, and gender. The index is useful for prospective transition
mapping, but its own framing distinguishes exposure and likely job
transformation from realized automation or displacement.

## Mechanisms

- Task-level occupational classification and worker survey input.
- Expert deliberation plus model-assisted scoring of occupational tasks.
- Exposure gradients rather than a binary automated/not-automated label.
- Linkage route to country microdata and distributional comparisons.

## Evidence

The reviewed page reports a 29,753-task classification base, 1,640 surveyed
workers, 52,558 data points for 2,861 tasks, and substantial cross-group and
cross-country variation. These are exposure estimates, not causal deployment
outcomes, forecasts of employment, or measures of wellbeing.

## Failure Modes

- Calling exposure realized automation, unemployment, or benefit.
- Ignoring task bundling, adoption, complementarity, institutions, and demand.
- Treating model-assisted scores as independent ground truth.
- Erasing group and country differences with a global aggregate.

## Claims To Add Or Update

- Preserve exposure, adoption, task change, job change, displacement, wages,
  welfare, and distribution as separate claims.
- Require affected-group denominators and adoption measurements.
- Do not infer forecasts, policy effectiveness, or support movement.

## Open Questions

- Which exposure scores predict actual changes under different institutions?
- How should informal, unpaid, care, and newly created work be measured?
- Which transition policies preserve capability, bargaining power, and access?
