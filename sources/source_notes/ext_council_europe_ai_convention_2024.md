## Thesis

The Convention provides a concrete international institutional comparator:
lifecycle duties, risk and impact assessment, procedural safeguards, remedies,
public authority, private actors, and a Conference of the Parties. It shows why
technical records alone cannot manufacture jurisdiction, legal force, remedy,
or democratic legitimacy.

## Mechanisms

- Technology-neutral lifecycle principles tied to human rights, democracy, and
  the rule of law.
- Iterative risk and impact assessment plus prevention and mitigation.
- Notice, information, complaint, challenge, and procedural safeguards.
- State implementation choices and a Conference of the Parties follow-up process.

## Evidence

The official page establishes the treaty's stated design and scope. It does not
show that parties have implemented it, that remedies work, that monitoring is
independent, or that any ASI Stack artifact is legally adequate.

## Failure Modes

- Presenting a schema mapping as treaty compliance.
- Ignoring exclusions, national-security boundaries, implementation choices,
  reservations, or changing ratification state.
- Treating risk assessment as mitigation or procedural availability as remedy.
- Importing legal conclusions without jurisdiction-specific expert review.

## Claims To Add Or Update

- Keep technical conformance, legal obligation, public authority, institutional
  monitoring, procedural remedy, and legitimacy separate.
- Include jurisdiction and implementation-state change as invalidation triggers.
- Do not claim legal compliance, treaty status, rights satisfaction, or safety.

## Open Questions

- Which technical receipts can support—but never replace—an institutional review?
- How are cross-border conflicts, redress, and enforcement failures escalated?
- Which public evidence is needed to evaluate implementation rather than text?
