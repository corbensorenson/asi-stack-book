## Reader Promise

After reading or hearing this companion note, a human should be able to follow
why the chapter treats compactness as a governed engineering bargain rather than
as a compression slogan. A compact core is useful only when the missing burden
is named, verified, repaired, paid for, or rejected.

## Dense Material Routed Here

| Dense item | Plain meaning | Boundary |
|---|---|---|
| `CompactGenerativeRecord` | The record that names the compact seed, generator, verifier, correction path, fallback, residuals, use envelope, costs, and promotion authority. | The record can make compactness inspectable; it does not prove compression utility or model quality. |
| Generate/verify/repair receipt | A reconstruction path where generation is checked against a declared target and mismatches become repair residuals, declared loss, fallback, quarantine, or negative evidence. | Exactness belongs to the final receipt, not to the generator output by itself. |
| Verifier separation | The evaluator must be separate enough from the generator for claim-relevant uses. | A self-grading generator can make compactness look better than it is. |
| Residual burden | Any missing detail, repair stream, metadata, human review, future computation, fallback storage, or governance work left after compaction. | If the residual is hidden, efficiency has been exported to another layer. |
| Literal fallback | The system keeps or returns to the expensive reference artifact when compact reconstruction is not cheaper, exact, or adequate. | Fallback is negative evidence for the route on that target, not a failure to hide. |
| Semantic Node Record | A scoped representation lease used only inside its grounding state, permitted uses, adequacy limits, and consumer policy. | It is not a canonical ontology verdict or proof of semantic adequacy. |

## Main Spine Must Keep

The reader chapter should not move these boundaries exclusively into companion
material:

- compactness requires seed, generator, verifier, repair, residual, fallback,
  policy, and cost accounting;
- failed verification blocks exactness promotion;
- semantic representations are leased under adequacy limits rather than
  treated as permanent truth;
- a compact route cannot claim model quality, downstream utility, memory
  savings, compression rate, context improvement, or deployment safety without
  its own evidence.

## Audio Treatment

In an audio script, do not read every record field as a long catalog. Narrate
the chapter as a simple discipline:

- a compact system is a promise to reconstruct or represent with less apparent
  structure;
- the promise is honest only when verifier, repair, fallback, and residual
  burden stay attached;
- a semantic node is a temporary representation lease, not the world itself;
- compactness that hides cost is not efficiency;
- the local proofs and fixtures check finite record boundaries, not broad
  compression performance.

Detailed field names can be routed to this companion note. The main audio
should keep the ordinary reader focused on the bargain: smaller is better only
when the missing cost has a named owner.
